3. Context & Problem Statement

A grocery website is also a planning tool.

Project Context

Safeway's current website mainly organizes shopping around grocery categories such as produce, meat, bakery, and deals. This case study explores how that experience could better support meal planning, recipe discovery, and grocery-list building.

Problem Statement

Many shoppers begin with meal goals such as "I need dinners for the week" rather than product categories. The original site structure did not fully match that planning behavior, creating a gap between the system model and the user's mental model.

6. Current Experience

The current model begins with product categories.

The existing experience asks users to navigate grocery aisles digitally. That structure can work for known-item shopping, but it is less direct for people planning meals, comparing recipes, or building a weekly list.

7. User Mental Model

Many shoppers start with meals, not aisles.

A shopper may think, "I am making pasta tonight," or "I need lunches for the week." Our redesign uses that planning mindset as the starting point, then connects meal ideas to recipes, ingredients, and cart actions.

8. Redesign Direction

From category browsing to meal planning.

The flow shifts from category, subcategory, and product browsing to intent, recipe, ingredients, and cart building.

9. Interface Metaphors

Using familiar planning objects to make actions clearer.

Recipe Card Board

Recipe cards make browsing meals feel familiar because users already understand them from cooking and meal planning.

Refrigerator Door

A virtual fridge organizes ingredients in a way that resembles how people already picture food storage at home.

Grocery List Notepad

A notepad with checkboxes maps to how people traditionally prepare shopping lists before going to the store.

10. Design Principles Applied

Principles translated into redesign choices.

Visibility

Issue: Important shopping paths and filters were not always immediately visible when they were hidden inside dropdowns or category menus.

Design Decision: We brought meal-planning actions, recipe categories, and shopping-list controls into the main experience so users could see where to begin without digging through extra navigation layers.

Design Rationale: Visibility was important because the redesign needed to make the user's next possible actions clear at a glance.

Constraints

Issue: The original category-based browsing flow presented many broad shopping paths at once, which could make it harder for users to decide where to start.

Design Decision: We organized the experience around a narrower meal-planning sequence: meal occasion, recipe, ingredients, and then cart.

Design Rationale: This used constraints by limiting the user's immediate choices to actions that matched their current goal, while still allowing them to browse more broadly later.

Feedback

Issue: Users needed clearer confirmation when recipes or ingredients were added to their list or cart.

Design Decision: We included visible feedback moments such as added states, checked items, loading indicators, and confirmation messages.

Design Rationale: Feedback helped users understand that the system had responded to their action and reduced uncertainty during the shopping flow.

Consistency

Issue: The original experience could feel inconsistent when categories, deals, recipes, and cart actions followed different interaction patterns.

Design Decision: We used consistent card layouts, button styles, labels, icons, and spacing across the meal-planning, recipe, and cart sections.

Design Rationale: Consistency made the interface easier to learn because users could recognize similar actions across different parts of the experience.

Signifiers / Affordances

Issue: Some interactive elements were not immediately clear as clickable or actionable.

Design Decision: We used clearer buttons, hover states, checkbox controls, tabs, and labeled cards to show what users could interact with.

Design Rationale: Stronger signifiers helped users understand the available actions without having to guess which elements were interactive.

Mapping

Issue: The relationship between meal choices, ingredients, and cart actions was not always obvious.

Design Decision: We placed controls close to the content they affected. Recipe cards were directly connected to ingredient lists and cart actions.

Design Rationale: Better mapping helped users understand how one action led to the next, from choosing a meal to building a grocery list.

Accessibility / Universal Design

Issue: Users with different abilities could struggle if text was small, contrast was weak, or navigation depended too heavily on visual discovery.

Design Decision: We used readable text, stronger contrast, alt text for images, keyboard-accessible controls, and responsive layouts.

Design Rationale: These choices made the redesign more usable for a wider range of users, including people using different devices, input methods, or accessibility needs.

11. Final Outcome

A clearer bridge between planning and shopping.

The redesign reframed Safeway from a product-category browsing experience into a meal-planning experience. Users could begin with what they wanted to cook, move into recipes, review ingredients, and connect those ingredients to cart actions. The final direction created a clearer relationship between planning meals and completing grocery shopping tasks.

12. Reflection and Lessons Learned

What this case study clarified.

This case study helped me understand that a grocery website is not only a product catalog; it also supports real-life planning behavior. The most important lesson was that a system's conceptual model should match how users already think about the task. Designing around meals instead of only categories made the experience feel more natural and showed how human-centered design can reduce confusion by aligning interface structure with user goals.
